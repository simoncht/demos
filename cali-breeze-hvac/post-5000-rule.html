The $5,000 Rule: How to Know Whether to Repair or Replace Your Air Conditioner

A practical, honest guide to one of the most common decisions homeowners face — and how our techs think through it.

What Is the $5,000 Rule?

The $5,000 Rule is a simple, widely-used heuristic in the HVAC world. The idea: if the cost to repair your current air conditioner approaches or exceeds $5,000, you're usually better off putting that money toward a new, more energy-efficient system instead.

If a repair is going to run you close to $5,000 — or more — you need to seriously ask whether you're just delaying the inevitable. A new system will often pay for itself faster than you'd expect.

That said, this rule isn't a magic number. It's a starting point, not a verdict. At Cali Breeze we never lead with a replacement recommendation — we look at the full picture first, because sometimes the right call really is a repair, and we'll tell you that straight.

The Four Factors We Weigh

Before we make any recommendation, our techs evaluate these four things together:

1. Age of Your System

Most central air conditioners are built to last 15–20 years with proper maintenance. If your unit is 10 years old or younger and is otherwise in decent shape, a repair usually makes sense — you still have meaningful life left in the system. Once you're past 12–15 years, the calculus shifts. An aging unit is more likely to develop secondary failures soon after one repair, meaning you could end up paying twice.

2. Repair History

One repair is a data point. Two or three repairs in the last few years is a pattern. If a system keeps asking for money, at some point you're throwing good money after bad. We look at what's already been spent and what's likely to come next before giving you our honest take.

3. Energy Efficiency and Operating Costs

Older systems run on older efficiency standards. A unit installed 12+ years ago may have a SEER rating of 10 or less; modern systems start at SEER 14–16 and go higher. An inefficient system isn't just aging — it's costing you on every power bill. When we factor in the savings a new system would deliver on your monthly bill, the payback period on replacement often looks a lot shorter than it first appears.

4. The Cost of the Specific Repair

Not all repairs are equal. Here's a realistic look at what common AC repairs cost in California:

Type of Repair Typical Cost Range Notes
Small repairs (capacitors, drain lines, thermostats) $150 – $500 Usually quick, affordable fixes worth doing at most ages
Major repairs (compressor, evaporator coil) $2,000 – $4,000+ High parts and labor cost — age of the unit matters a lot here

The compressor deserves special mention. It's the heart of your cooling system — it circulates refrigerant between the evaporator and condenser coils — and it can run up to $4,000 or more to replace. On a unit that's 12+ years old, that repair alone gets you deep into "$5,000 rule" territory before you factor in any other work the system might need.

How to Apply the Rule Yourself

A straightforward way to use this as a gut-check:

  • Get the repair estimate from a licensed tech (that's us — free estimate, no pressure).
  • Multiply the repair cost by the age of your unit in years.
  • If that number exceeds $5,000, replacement is likely the smarter long-term move.
  • If it's well under $5,000 — and the unit is younger — go ahead and repair.

For example: a $400 capacitor swap on a 7-year-old unit gives you $2,800 — clearly repair territory. A $2,800 compressor replacement on a 14-year-old unit gives you $39,200 — clearly replacement territory, even before you factor in efficiency.

We will never push you toward a replacement you don't need. If a repair is the right call, that's what we'll tell you — straight.

How Routine Maintenance Changes the Equation

One of the best things you can do for your AC system is keep up with annual maintenance. A well-maintained unit runs more efficiently, lasts longer, and is far less likely to spring a major repair on you at the worst possible time. Our maintenance visits typically include:

  • Checking and cleaning coils (dirty coils are a top cause of reduced cooling and compressor strain)
  • Inspecting refrigerant levels and electrical connections
  • Lubricating moving parts and testing capacitors
  • Catching small issues — a worn contact, a developing leak — before they become $2,000 problems

The goal is simple: catch the small stuff early so you're never faced with a surprise $3,500 repair on a 13-year-old system. That's the kind of situation where the $5,000 Rule really bites.
